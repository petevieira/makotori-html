Detachment

  • C'est vrai que d'être détaché par rapport aux émotions veut dire que je les observe et je les vois comme je suis spectateur, intéressé. Et en même temps je ne les laissent pas toujours prendre leur course. Parfois, je décide qu'elles ne servent plus de m'exprimer ou de m'informer et on traverse à la souffrance inutile. Inutile parce que les émotions, à ce temps-là, ignorent la vérité de la situation, ils sont dans le déni. Si on a la deuil pendant quelque temps après la mort d'une proche, ça fait du bien psychologiquement et physiquement de la laisser sortir. En revanche, si on continue de souffrir, c'est parce qu'on veut pas accepter la vérité que cette personne est morte. C'est de plus vivre dans la réalité. Ou de ne pas accepter que le monde est aléatoire est que de mauvaises choses peuvent arriver à n'importe qui n'importe quand. Moi, j'aime la réalité parce que c'est toujours fiable. Beaucoup de souffrance vient de l'imagination du fait qu'on perçoit et judge contrairement à la réalité qui existe devant nous.
  • Si je trouve ma copine en train d'embrasser un autre garçon, c'est nul qu'elle m'en a pas parlé, mais la réalité c'est qu'elle a des sentiments pour ce gar, et si je suis en colère ou dans le déni, rien ne change le fait qu'elle a ces sentiments. Alors, c'est inutile d'avoir beaucoup d'émotions pendant du temps, parce que c'est de ne pas accepter la réalité et la vérité. Vaut mieux investiguer et parler avec elle pour mieux comprendre la situation.
  • Peut-être d'être détaché c'est d'être attaché à la réalité au point que bien que les situations peuvent être mauvaises et doivent être changés, la plupart de mes émotions serviront à rien pour changer cette réalité. Et c'est seulement avec une vue claire de la réalité qu'on peut approcher un enjeu avec une chance à réussir.
  • Mais, dans une discussion de détachement, on dirait que le sujet to point de vue, spécifiquement l'optimisme, est essentiel. Si on pense toujours que les choses vont tomber pire qu'avant ou qu'on ne va pas en sortir des situations,
  • Il faut accepter que toutes choses sont en train de changer sans arrêt. Le plus que tu tiens sur l'idée que quelque chose dois rester comme ça le plus de problèmes tu auras. Si quelqu'un gratte ta voiture, c'est juste qu'elle a suivi son chemin plus vite d'être nouvelle à être vieille. Mais le changement c'est normal. A n'importe quel moment tu me demande comment ça va, je peux toujours répondre que je me sens bien. Mais c'est quoi 'bien' ? C'est quoi 'mauvais' ? Pour certains, 'bien' c'est quand les émotions positives augments ou sont augmenté, est 'mauvais' c'est quand les émotions descendent ou sont bas. Mais imaginez si tout avec tout ça, peut importe si les émotions sont hauts ou bas, en augmentation ou en descent, avec tout ça tu peux répondre 'je me sens bien', j'ai la paix interne. Et du coup tu te sens équilibré. Le voie du milieu n'est pas la voie sans être aux extremes, c'est la voie avec une moyenne qui se trouve au milieu. Pas trop de relaxation, pas trop de stress. Tu ne reste ni dans l'une ni dans l'autre.1

Interdependence

  • The healthiest way we can interact with those close to us is by being truly interdependent. This is where two people, both strong individuals, are involved with each other, but without sacrificing themselves or compromising their values.2
    • Strong individual: to me this means being self-confident, having integrity, knowing more or less what you want while being open to learning and change.
    • Not sacrificing oneself: to me this means not finding yourself doing things that you wouldn't normally just to satisfy your partner. Trying new things is fine because you would normally do that. But if trying new drugs is normally extremely against your values for reasons such as legality, health, crowd influences, etc., then you after sacrificing yourself to be with the other person and not upset them. You are no longer showing your true self and therefore no longer being honest.
    • Compromising values: to me this means letting go of values in order to please the other person and make the relationship work. But if those values are important to you, is sacrificing them going to make the relationship work? You aren't standing up for what you believe or what you want. Eventually, you find that you have turned into a different person that wouldn't couldn't have imagined before.

Crying during movies

  • I think what triggers me most to start really crying is when people make actions and reactions of integrity, love and compassion (when it's easy to react with anger, revenge, hate), because it always results in making a positive impact on people and the world. And it's often so lacking in our actual world.

Routine

  • If we start off from the idea that we want to be present all the time to avoid worry, stress, regret, wondering what if things had gone differently, then something a routine can provide is getting rid of the need to think about when I'm gonna do this or that, if I have time for that, and instead just you can just be focused on what you're doing, knowing that the other things that you want/need to do in your day will take place, regardless of whether you think about them.

Notes - The First and Last Freedom - Jiddu Krishnamurti

  • An education that teaches us not how but what to think is an education that calls for a governing class of pastors and masters. But “the very idea of leading somebody is anti-social and anti-spiritual”. To the man who exercises it, leadership brings gratification of the craving for power; to those who are led, it brings the gratification of the desire for certainty and security.
  • When you quote the Bhagavad Gita,or the Bible, or some Chinese Sacred Book, surely you are merely repeating, are you not? And what you are repeating is not the truth. It is a lie; for truth cannot be repeated.
  • From prayer we pass to yoga, and yoga, we find, is another of the things which Krishnamurti does not offer. For yoga is concentration, and concentration is exclusion. “You build a wall of resistance by concentration on a thought which you have chosen, and you try to ward off all the others.” What is commonly called meditation is merely “the cultivation of resistance, of exclusive concentration on an idea of our choice”. But what makes you choose? “What makes you say this is good, true, noble, and the rest is not? Obviously the choice is based on pleasure, reward or achievement; or it is merely a reaction of one’s conditioning or tradition. Why do you choose at all? Why not examine every thought? When you are interested in the many, why choose one? Why not examine every interest? Instead of creating resistance, why not go into each interest as it arises, and not merely concentrate on one idea, one interest? After all, you are made up of many interests, you have many masks, consciously and unconsciously. Why choose one and discard all the others, in combating which you spend all your energies, thereby creating resistance, conflict and friction. Whereas if you consider every thought as it arises—every thought, not just a few thoughts—then there is no exclusion. But it is an arduous thing to examine every thought. Because, as you are looking at one thought, another slips in. But if you are aware without domination or justification, you will see that, by merely looking at that thought, no other thought intrudes. It is only when you condemn, compare, approximate, that other thoughts enter in.”
  • “Judge not that ye be not judged.” The gospel precept applies to our dealings with ourselves no less than to our dealings with others. Where there is judgement, where there is comparison and condemnation, openness of mind is absent; there can be no freedom from the tyranny of symbols and systems, no escape from the past and the environment. Introspection with a predetermined purpose, self-examination within the framework of some traditional code, some set of hallowed postulates—these do not, these cannot help us.
  • Where there is judgement, where there is comparison and condemnation, openness of mind is absent; there can be no freedom from the tyranny of symbols and systems, no escape from the past and the environment. Introspection with a predetermined purpose, self-examination within the framework of some traditional code, some set of hallowed postulates—these do not, these cannot help us. There is a transcendent spontaneity of life, a ‘creative Reality’, as Krishnamurti calls it, which reveals itself as immanent only when the perceiver’s mind is in a state of ‘alert passivity’, of ‘choiceless awareness’. Judgement and comparison commit us irrevocably to duality. Only choiceless awareness can lead to non-duality, to the reconciliation of opposites in a total understanding and a total love.
    • This choiceless awareness—at every moment and in all the circumstances of life—is the only effective meditation
